Introduction of Natural Lamguage Processing

Natural Language Processing (NLP) is a branch of Artificial Intelligence and Computer Science that enables computers to understand, analyze, process, and generate human language. Human language is complex because the same word can have different meanings depending on context, grammar, emotion, and sentence structure. NLP tries to bridge the gap between human communication and machine understanding.
The main goal of NLP is to allow computers to work with text and speech in the same way humans do. Applications of NLP include chatbots, machine translation, sentiment analysis, spam filtering, search engines, recommendation systems, speech recognition, and large language models like GPT.
An NLP system usually follows a pipeline. The first step is collecting text data, often called a corpus. A corpus can contain documents, tweets, reviews, books, PDFs, or conversations. Once the text is collected, preprocessing begins. Raw text usually contains punctuation, unnecessary spaces, symbols, stop words, and inconsistent capitalization. Cleaning the text improves the quality of the NLP model.
One of the first preprocessing techniques is tokenization. Tokenization means splitting text into smaller units called tokens. Tokens may be words, sentences, or characters. For example, the sentence “I love NLP” can be split into the tokens “I”, “love”, and “NLP”. Tokenization is important because machines cannot directly process complete sentences efficiently. Most NLP models work with tokens.
Another preprocessing step is removing stop words. Stop words are common words such as “is”, “the”, “and”, and “a”. These words appear very frequently but often contribute little meaning to many NLP tasks. Removing them can reduce noise and improve efficiency. However, in some applications like translation or question answering, stop words may still be important.
Stemming and lemmatization are techniques used to reduce words to their root form. Stemming removes suffixes mechanically. For example, “playing”, “played”, and “plays” may all become “play”. However, stemming is not always linguistically correct. Lemmatization is more advanced because it converts a word into its meaningful dictionary form, known as the lemma. For example, “better” becomes “good” and “running” becomes “run”. Lemmatization usually gives more accurate results than stemming.
Part-of-Speech tagging, often called POS tagging, identifies the grammatical role of words in a sentence. A word may act as a noun, verb, adjective, adverb, or pronoun depending on context. For example, in the sentence “Apple is beautiful”, “Apple” is a noun and “beautiful” is an adjective. POS tagging helps NLP systems understand sentence structure and grammar.
Named Entity Recognition (NER) is another important NLP task. NER identifies real-world entities such as people, organizations, locations, dates, and products from text. For example, in the sentence “Microsoft was founded in America”, “Microsoft” is identified as an organization and “America” as a location. NER is widely used in search engines, recommendation systems, and information extraction.
Computers cannot understand raw text directly because machine learning models operate on numbers. Therefore, NLP converts text into numerical representations called vectors. This process is known as vectorization.
One traditional vectorization technique is Bag of Words (BoW). Bag of Words creates a vocabulary of all unique words and represents each document using word frequencies. For example, if the vocabulary contains the words “I”, “love”, “AI”, and “Python”, each sentence becomes a numerical vector indicating how many times each word appears. Bag of Words is simple but has limitations because it ignores grammar, order, and context.
TF-IDF, which stands for Term Frequency–Inverse Document Frequency, improves upon Bag of Words. TF-IDF gives higher importance to words that are rare and meaningful while reducing the importance of very common words. For example, words like “the” or “is” receive low scores, while domain-specific words receive higher scores. TF-IDF is widely used in search engines and document retrieval systems.
Word embeddings were introduced to solve the limitations of sparse representations like BoW and TF-IDF. Word embeddings represent words as dense numerical vectors in a continuous vector space. Similar words have similar vector representations. For example, the vectors for “king” and “queen” may be close in space because they share semantic meaning. Popular embedding techniques include Word2Vec, GloVe, and FastText.
Traditional NLP relied heavily on manual feature engineering and statistical methods. Engineers had to manually design features such as word frequencies, sentence lengths, or grammar rules. Modern NLP uses deep learning, which automatically learns patterns from large amounts of data.
A major breakthrough in NLP came with the Transformer architecture, introduced in the paper Attention Is All You Need. Transformers use a mechanism called attention. Attention allows the model to focus on important words in a sentence while processing language. Unlike older models that processed text sequentially, transformers can process all words simultaneously, making them faster and better at understanding context.
The attention mechanism helps models understand relationships between words. For example, in the sentence “The animal did not cross the road because it was tired”, the word “it” refers to “animal”. Attention mechanisms learn such relationships automatically.
One important transformer-based model is BERT. BERT reads text bidirectionally, meaning it considers both left and right context simultaneously. Earlier models usually processed text only from left to right. Because of bidirectional understanding, BERT performs very well on tasks such as question answering, sentiment analysis, and text classification.
Another important model family is GPT. GPT models are mainly designed for text generation. They predict the next word repeatedly to generate coherent sentences and paragraphs. GPT models are used in chatbots, coding assistants, summarization systems, and conversational AI.
Sentiment analysis is a popular NLP application that determines emotional tone or opinion in text. A sentence such as “I love this movie” is classified as positive, while “This phone is terrible” is classified as negative. Libraries such as spaCy and TextBlob are commonly used for sentiment analysis in Python.
Modern NLP systems also use Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). RAG combines information retrieval with large language models. In RAG systems, documents are split into chunks, converted into embeddings, and stored in vector databases such as FAISS or Chroma. When a user asks a question, relevant chunks are retrieved and passed to the language model to generate accurate answers. Frameworks such as LangChain help developers build RAG applications.
Several Python libraries are widely used in NLP. NLTK is commonly used for educational and traditional NLP tasks. spaCy is faster and more suitable for industrial applications. Transformers provides access to pretrained transformer models like BERT and GPT. scikit-learn is widely used for machine learning algorithms such as TF-IDF and classification.
In practical NLP coding projects, the workflow usually involves loading text data, cleaning and preprocessing the text, converting text into vectors or embeddings, training or using a model, and finally generating predictions or responses. Modern NLP applications increasingly rely on transformer models because they provide strong contextual understanding and better performance than traditional statistical methods.
In summary, NLP is the technology that allows computers to process human language through preprocessing, vectorization, machine learning, embeddings, attention mechanisms, and transformer-based deep learning models. Traditional NLP focused on rules and statistical representations, while modern NLP focuses on contextual understanding through transformers and large language models.
